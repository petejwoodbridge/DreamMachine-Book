Steam clarifies its rules on AI disclosure in games
Valve has quietly tightened up how Steam talks about generative AI —
not by reversing course, but by making the rules easier to interpret (and
easier to comply with).
The key change is a sharper distinction between AI used as a behind-
the-scenes productivity boost and AI used to create content that players
actually experience . In other words: Steam isnʼt trying to police whether
your team used a coding assistant or other “efficiency” tooling during
development — it wants transparency about what ends up in the
game , on the store page, or in the games marketing.
That means if GenAI touches the final art, audio, text, or other player-
facing elements, it should be disclosed ; if itʼs purely internal workflow
support (think code helpers, admin tools, ideation aids), it doesnʼt need
to be surfaced to customers via Steamʼs disclosure section.
Valve is also keeping its two-bucket framing for how AI shows up in
games: pre-generated content (created during development and
shipped as part of the release) and live-generated content (created by
AI systems during gameplay). The latter comes with extra scrutiny:
developers are expected to describe the “guardrails” in place to prevent
illegal content from being generated, and Valve has added a Steam
overlay reporting option so players can flag problematic live-generated
outputs.
This lands right in the middle of a broader games industry argument
about whether AI labelling is even meaningful. Epic CEO Tim Sweeney
has been blunt: heʼs argued that “the AI tag” makes sense for authorship
or licensing contexts, but not for game stores, because AI will be
involved in “nearly all future production” anyway . Valveʼs revised
approach looks like a compromise between those poles — keeping
disclosure as a consumer-facing transparency tool, but narrowing it to
the moments where AI actually touches the shipped experience , rather
than turning every modern software workflow into a compliance
checkbox.
