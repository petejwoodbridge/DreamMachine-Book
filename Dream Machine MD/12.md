Editors Pick
88% of Creators Said “No”
The UK government has just published a progress report on Copyright
& AI , and while itʼs light on decisions, the signal from artists and creators
is loud and clear. This is the official checkpoint before new rules land
in March 2026 , when the government must set out how AI models can
(or canʼt) use copyrighted work.
The consultation drew around 11,500 responses in just a couple of
months. Musicians, writers, filmmakers, publishers and other creatives
turned up in force, making this one of the biggest copyright
consultations the UK has ever seen. That alone tells you how high the
stakes are.

When asked what should happen, creators were decisive. 88% said AI
training should require licences in all cases. Almost no one supported
a free-for-all. The message was simple: if your work helps train an AI
system, you should have a say — and be paid . Transparency and opt-
outs werenʼt enough for most people.
The government is now working with 50+ experts from across music,
film, games and AI to figure out what comes next. The real battle is about
control: who decides how creative work is used, who gets paid, and
whether AI becomes a partner to culture — or just another extraction
machine.
The next report in early 2026 is where those decisions get real. Read
the [Full Statement of Progress here](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/copyright-and-artificial-intelligence-progress-report/copyright-and-artificial-intelligence-statement-of-progress-under-section-137-data-use-and-access-act)
