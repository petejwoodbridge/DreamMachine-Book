Editors Pick
Sundance 2026: AI isnʼt just a theme — itʼs now part of
the festivalʼs infrastructure
This year at Sundance, AI shows up in two places at once: on screen as
subject matter , and off screen as the new production layer indie
filmmakers are being asked to understand (and sometimes adopt). The
result is a festival where the AI conversation feels less like sci-fi
speculation and more like a live negotiation over power, craft, labour,
and the future “default” toolchain.
On the films-about-AI side, the headline is The AI Doc: Or How I
Became an Apocaloptimist — a directorʼs-eye tour through the
spectrum from accelerationist optimism to existential risk , featuring
major AI figures and critics, and landing on the uneasy conclusion that
the train is moving fast whether we like it or not ( [LINK](https://www.theguardian.com/film/2026/jan/27/sundance-ai-documentary-daniel-roher?utm_source=chatgpt.com) ). Alongside
it, Ghost in the Machine digs into AIʼs origin story as something shaped

But the more consequential Sundance signal may be the institutional
shift : Sundance Institute is using the festival to launch a product-
agnostic AI Literacy push designed to help filmmakers make informed
choices about whether/how to use AI — including free learning via
Sundance Collab, community conversations, and a new fellowship and
alliance model ( [LINK](https://www.sundance.org/blogs/centering-the-artist-why-were-launching-the-ai-literacy-initiative/) ). Backing that, Google has put $2M into Sundance
Institute with the stated aim of training 100,000+ artists in foundational
AI skills, in collaboration with partners like The Gotham and Film
Independent ( [LINK](https://blog.google/company-news/outreach-and-initiatives/google-org/sundance-institute-ai-education/) ). This is a notable repositioning: not “hereʼs a tool,”
but “hereʼs a literacy layer and shared standards so artists can stay in
the driverʼs seat.”
The AI conversation has shifted from “cool demo” to the nuances of
“legal + ethical operating system” and "literacy"— with talks that keep
circling the same pressure points: what counts as human authorship in
an AI-assisted workflow, what evidence you need to prove it (logs,
transparency, documentation), and where copyright risk actually sits
when models are trained on scraped work . The Instituteʼs newly
launched AI Literacy Initiative frames this as creator empowerment (and
guardrails) rather than hype, while Story Forum sessions this week lean
into the practicalities — including a “Legal Toolkit for Producers Using
AI” , and curriculum that explicitly tackles questions like whether AI-
generated content can be copyrighted and how to protect your project
in a world of contested datasets. ( [LINK](https://www.sundance.org/blogs/centering-the-artist-why-were-launching-the-ai-literacy-initiative/) )
Then there are the festival-floor innovations and showcases : Google
turns up in the programme with a partner event focused on “Flow,” its AI
filmmaking tool framed around artistic control (including screenings and
discussion, and a few hints about things that might make it into the next
VEO models), plus broader storytelling/AI sessions in the mix ( [LINK](https://festival.sundance.org/program/partner-event/696017897cf1ef21017dad97) ).
This matters because itʼs not just demos — itʼs the attempt to
normalise AI as a legitimate part of the creative process inside the
cultural institutions that have historically defined indie credibility.
Finally, product launches : Adobe used Sundance week to roll out
updates positioned as workflow accelerants rather than “replace the
artist” magic tricks — notably AI-powered masking/object selection
improvements in Premiere, plus major After Effects upgrades across
animation, 3D and performance ( [LINK](https://www.digitalcameraworld.com/photography/video-editing/major-adobe-premiere-and-after-effects-update-streamlines-advanced-video-editing-workflows-with-new-tools-and-ai?utm_source=chatgpt.com) ). Adobe also doubled down on
talent pipeline + access: Ignite Day at Sundance (for emerging creators)
and an Adobe Film & TV Fund approach that explicitly includes support
for filmmakers integrating AI into their workflows ( [LINK](https://blog.adobe.com/en/publish/2026/01/20/sundance-film-festival-2026-creativity-community-power-storytelling) ). In other
words: AI isnʼt just a controversy to manage — itʼs becoming a funding
category, a skills track, and a competitive advantage.
Editorʼs take: The tone of Sundance 2026 is where AI starts to feel less
like a debate about whether it belongs in filmmaking, and more like a
debate about who sets the terms . The festival is effectively becoming a
battleground for AI literacy, ethics, and access — right as the
mainstream toolchains and software ships AI features by default, and the
indie sector tries to protect creative agency..... without the pressure of
falling behind.
